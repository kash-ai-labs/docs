## Overview

This page gives you a compact working reference for **Kashmiri grammar**. It is designed for learners, annotators, translators, and builders who need a fast structural overview instead of a full academic grammar.

The most important baseline ideas are:

- Kashmiri commonly uses **subject-object-verb** order.
- Kashmiri nouns belong to **masculine** or **feminine** gender.
- Kashmiri distinguishes **singular** and **plural**.
- **Case marking** and **postpositions** carry a lot of meaning.
- Verbs can change with **gender**, **number**, and construction type.

## Core rules

### Rule 1 — Learn nouns with gender

Kashmiri nouns belong to one of two grammatical genders:

- **masculine**
- **feminine**

You should learn noun gender as part of the word itself, because gender affects agreement behavior.

### Rule 2 — Expect singular and plural

Kashmiri distinguishes:

- **singular**
- **plural**

Plural formation is not fully uniform across all nouns. Some patterns are regular, but not every noun behaves the same way.

### Rule 3 — Verb-final order is the safest baseline

A common default sentence pattern is:

```text
Subject + Object + Verb
```

This means the verb often comes at the end of the clause.

### Rule 4 — Cases matter

Kashmiri meaning is not carried by word order alone. Nouns and pronouns can appear in different **case forms**, especially in possession, location, indirect object, and past constructions.

## Nouns and agreement

### Gender

Every noun is treated as masculine or feminine. This is grammatical gender, not just natural gender.

### Number

Every noun can be singular or plural, but plural patterns can differ by noun type.

### Agreement

Gender and number can influence:

- adjectives
- pronouns
- verbs

That makes agreement a central part of grammatical accuracy.

## Cases and postpositions

Kashmiri uses several important case relations. A practical learner-level summary is below.

| Case or function | Practical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Nominative | basic subject form |
| Dative | to, for, indirect object, experiencer-like roles |
| Genitive or possessive | of, belonging to |
| Locative | in, at, near, location relations |
| Agentive or ergative-like role | important in some past or perfective constructions |

### Postpositions

Like Urdu and Hindi, Kashmiri relies heavily on **postpositions**.

That means the marker usually comes **after** the noun phrase, not before it.

A useful mental template is:

```text
Noun or pronoun + case form + postposition
```

## Pronouns

Kashmiri grammar sources describe a rich pronoun system, including:

- personal pronouns
- possessive pronouns
- demonstrative pronouns
- interrogative pronouns
- relative and reflexive forms

### Demonstratives

Kashmiri is often described as having a **three-term demonstrative system**, roughly corresponding to:

- this
- that
- that farther away or out of sight

That makes deixis richer than a simple English two-way distinction.

## Verbs

Verb behavior is one of the most important parts of Kashmiri grammar.

### Agreement

Verb forms can reflect:

- gender
- number
- sometimes person
- construction type

### Past constructions

Some past and perfective constructions show **agentive** or **ergative-like** behavior. This means agreement may not always follow a simple subject-only rule.

### Practical rule

When building a sentence, decide these in order:

1. what the main verb is
2. whether the sentence is present, past, or another form
3. whether the clause needs a special case-marked subject or object
4. what agreement the verb should show

## Common sentence patterns

These patterns are good safe defaults.

### Pattern 1

```text
Subject + Object + Verb
```

### Pattern 2

```text
Subject + Postpositional phrase + Verb
```

### Pattern 3

```text
Subject + Object + Postpositional phrase + Verb
```

### Pattern 4

```text
Possessor phrase + Noun + Predicate
```

| Past tense | watch for agentive or ergative-like behavior |

## Practical learner notes

### If you are starting from scratch

Use these as your default workflow:

1. learn nouns together with gender
2. place the verb at the end
3. check whether a case-marked form is needed
4. add postpositions after the noun phrase
5. confirm verb agreement at the end

### If you are building examples for annotation or teaching

Use short examples first:

```text
subject + object + verb
subject + location phrase + verb
subject + indirect object + object + verb
```

Then increase complexity gradually.
