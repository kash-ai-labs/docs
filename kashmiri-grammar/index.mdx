## Overview

This page gives you a compact working reference for **Kashmiri grammar**. It is designed for learners, annotators, translators, and builders who need a fast structural overview instead of a full academic grammar.

The current version also folds in a few high-value takeaways from standard grammar books, especially **Modern Kashmiri Grammar** by Omkar N. Koul and Kashi Wali and **A Grammar of the Kashmiri Language** by T. R. Wade.

The most important baseline ideas are:

- Kashmiri commonly uses **subject-object-verb** order as a safe learner baseline.
- Modern grammars also note a **verb-second tendency** in parts of Kashmiri syntax.
- Kashmiri nouns belong to **masculine** or **feminine** gender.
- Kashmiri distinguishes **singular** and **plural**.
- **Case marking** and **postpositions** carry a lot of meaning.
- Verbs can change with **gender**, **number**, and construction type.
- Some verbs take **dative subjects**, which is a major pattern learners should notice early.

## Core rules

### Rule 1 — Learn nouns with gender

Kashmiri nouns belong to one of two grammatical genders:

- **masculine**
- **feminine**

You should learn noun gender as part of the word itself, because gender affects agreement behavior.

### Rule 2 — Expect singular and plural

Kashmiri distinguishes:

- **singular**
- **plural**

Plural formation is not fully uniform across all nouns. Some patterns are regular, but not every noun behaves the same way.

### Rule 3 — Verb-final order is the safest baseline, but not the whole story

A common default sentence pattern is:

```text
Subject + Object + Verb
```

This means the verb often comes at the end of the clause.

At the same time, **Modern Kashmiri Grammar** highlights Kashmiri's **verb-second** behavior as one of the language's distinctive features. So for practical work, use SOV as your safe starter pattern, but do not assume every well-formed clause is a simple plain verb-final line.

### Rule 4 — Cases matter

Kashmiri meaning is not carried by word order alone. Nouns and pronouns can appear in different **case forms**, especially in possession, location, indirect object, and past constructions.

## Nouns and agreement

### Gender

Every noun is treated as masculine or feminine. This is grammatical gender, not just natural gender.

### Number

Every noun can be singular or plural, but plural patterns can differ by noun type.

### Agreement

Gender and number can influence:

- adjectives
- pronouns
- verbs

That makes agreement a central part of grammatical accuracy.

## Cases and postpositions

Kashmiri uses several important case relations. A practical learner-level summary is below.

| Case or function | Practical meaning |
| --- | --- |
| Nominative | basic subject form |
| Dative | to, for, indirect object, experiencer-like roles |
| Genitive or possessive | of, belonging to |
| Locative | in, at, near, location relations |
| Agentive or ergative-like role | important in some past or perfective constructions |

### Postpositions

Like Urdu and Hindi, Kashmiri relies heavily on **postpositions**.

That means the marker usually comes **after** the noun phrase, not before it.

A useful mental template is:

```text
Noun or pronoun + case form + postposition
```

## Pronouns

Kashmiri grammar sources describe a rich pronoun system, including:

- personal pronouns
- possessive pronouns
- demonstrative pronouns
- interrogative pronouns
- relative and reflexive forms

### Demonstratives

Kashmiri is often described as having a **three-term demonstrative system**, roughly corresponding to:

- this
- that
- that farther away or out of sight

That makes deixis richer than a simple English two-way distinction.

A useful detail from **Modern Kashmiri Grammar** is that the extra demonstrative **ti** is used for **"that (out of sight)"** with inanimate nouns.

### Status and honorific usage

The grammar books also note a **status distinction** in pronoun use. In practice, plural pronoun forms can be used honorifically for singular addressees, which makes politeness part of the grammar and not just vocabulary choice.

## Verbs

Verb behavior is one of the most important parts of Kashmiri grammar.

### Agreement

Verb forms can reflect:

- gender
- number
- sometimes person
- construction type

### Past constructions

Some past and perfective constructions show **agentive** or **ergative-like** behavior. This means agreement may not always follow a simple subject-only rule.

### Dative-subject verbs

One especially important lesson from **Modern Kashmiri Grammar** is the class of **dative verbs** or **psych verbs**. In these constructions:

- the subject is marked **dative**
- the theme or affected noun can remain **nominative**
- the verb agrees with that nominative element

This pattern is especially common with meanings such as:

- knowing
- feeling
- wanting
- being able
- recalling
- mental or physical states

For learners and translators, this is one of the highest-value patterns to watch for, because literal subject mapping from English can fail here.

### Practical rule

When building a sentence, decide these in order:

1. what the main verb is
2. whether the sentence is present, past, or another form
3. whether the clause needs a special case-marked subject or object
4. what agreement the verb should show

## Common sentence patterns

These patterns are good safe defaults.

### Pattern 1

### Pattern 2


### Pattern 3


### Pattern 4

## Book-based learnings worth remembering

These are some of the most useful points that stand out once you compare the main grammar books.

### 1. Kashmiri is not just "Hindi-Urdu with different words"

The modern grammars stress that Kashmiri has structural traits that stand out even within Indo-Aryan, especially its **verb-second tendencies**, its rich case behavior, and its agreement patterns.

### 2. Possession can trigger agreement behavior

Wade's grammar repeatedly shows possessive marking that agrees with the possessed noun in **gender** and **number**. That means possession is not always a simple invariable linker.

### 3. Pronoun systems carry distance and status

The books make it clear that pronouns do more than point to person. They also encode:

- nearness vs distance
- visible vs out-of-sight reference in some demonstrative contrasts
- honorific or status-sensitive usage

### 4. Dative experiencers are core grammar, not edge cases

If a sentence means *know*, *feel*, *want*, *remember*, or a similar state, check carefully whether Kashmiri uses a **dative subject** rather than a plain nominative one.

### 5. Agreement must be checked construction by construction

The books do not support a simplistic rule like "the verb always agrees with the subject." In practice, learners need to check the clause type, especially in past constructions and dative-verb patterns.

## Practical learner notes

### If you are starting from scratch

Use these as your default workflow:

1. learn nouns together with gender
2. place the verb at the end
3. check whether a case-marked form is needed
4. add postpositions after the noun phrase
5. confirm verb agreement at the end

### If you are building examples for annotation or teaching

Use short examples first:

```text
subject + object + verb
subject + location phrase + verb
subject + indirect object + object + verb
```

Then increase complexity gradually.
